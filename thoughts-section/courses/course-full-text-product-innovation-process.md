### 2.4 Product Requirements □

### 2.5.1 Product Functional Requirements □

Product Functional Requirements refer to the specific features, capabilities, and behaviours that a Product must possess to meet the needs of its users. These requirements typically describe the functional aspects of the Product, such as the tasks it should be able to perform, the actions it should be able to take, and the results it should produce. These are feature-specific requirements.

Must-have: requirements that determine the project success
Should have: requirements that add value
Nice to have: requirements that add convenience
GPT Prompt suggestion (be sure to provide context)
“Act as a Product Manager. Given a Product that I described earlier, list all possible functional requirements and categorise them by Must-Have, Should-Have, and Nice-To-Have functional requirements.”

### 2.5.2 Product Non-Functional Requirements □

Product Non-Functional Requirements refer to the attributes that a Product must possess to ensure its usability, reliability, security, and performance. These requirements typically describe the non-functional aspects of the Product, such as its user interface, scalability, availability, and maintainability. Non-functional requirements may include factors such as response time, security, accessibility, usability, and reliability, among others. Briefly describe them here for now, they will be finalised in the Beta testing stage.

Compatibility: What are the platforms and systems the solution must be compatible with (e.g., Browser version X and above, or Windows version Y and later). Does the User (agency/client) require interaction with other systems using APIs or certain programming languages? What document formats are needed by the User? If this is a new version of an existing Product, what are the compatibility needs of the User?

Security: What are the User’s security needs? Does the customer require the data to be encrypted? What type of standards do they need to comply with? (If there is a legal or some external requirement mandating this customer need, describe it in the section Legal, Regulatory, and Compliance.)

Operational: Does the User depend on our internal operations, such as the solution being maintained as a software service? Does the User need occur Mon-Fri during business hours, or 24/7?

Internationalisation: What languages and in what countries will the proposed Product be used in? Are there market-specific requirements?

Documentation: What forms and types of documentation are needed for Product usage? This may include a User manual in printed or on-line format. Users may need a one-page quick start guide.

Support: What are the support needs placed on us? What does the User expect from support (e.g. email responses, phone calls)?

Legal, Regulatory, and Compliance: Check with the legal team for all of the legal and regulatory requirements needed by the User and document them here.

Miscellaneous: any other Product non-functional requirements that you can foresee.

### 2.5.3 Turning Requirements into User Stories □

In order to actually develop the requirements that you’ve made, you now need to translate them into a language fit for developers. Furthermore, since you will be working in an Agile manner, the requirements you give to developers will need to be in the form of User Stories. User stories are a fundamental element in Agile Product management and development, serving as a simplified description of a feature or requirement from the perspective of the end-user or customer. They focus on the type of user, what they want, and why, helping to create a user-focused mindset for development teams.

## Here’s how to approach User Stories:

## User-Centric:

- Begin with the type of user who requires the feature (e.g., “As a [type of user]”).
- This approach ensures the feature is grounded in the needs of real users, helping to maintain a user-centric approach to development.

## Desire/Need:

- Clearly state what the user wants to achieve (e.g., “I want [an action]”).
*This keeps the conversation anchored around specific functionalities, not technical implementations.

## Benefit or Value:

- Clarify the underlying reason or benefit for the desired action (e.g., “so that [benefit/value]”).

### 2.9 Product Key Performance Indicators □

Describe the performance measures that should be used to gauge the project’s business outcomes for key processes and services. Product KPIs can vary depending on the industry, market, and Product type, but generally include a mix of quantitative and qualitative measures that track different aspects of Product performance.

## Some common Product KPIs include:

- Sales revenue: measures the amount of revenue generated by the Product over a given period of time.
- Customer acquisition cost: measures the cost of acquiring a new customer for the Product, including marketing and sales expenses.
- Customer lifetime value: measures the total value a customer brings to the Product over the entire duration of their relationship with the company.
- Churn rate: measures the rate at which customers discontinue using the Product or unsubscribe from the service.
- Net promoter score: measures the level of customer satisfaction and likelihood to recommend the Product to others.
- Time to market: measures the time it takes to launch a new Product or update an existing one.

### 5.3 Continuous Iteration □

### 5.3.1 Sprint Planning and Prioritisation

By now, your chosen project management tool should contain a history of your User Stories, complete with the relevant Feature they sit in. All of your Features could be under one Epic which represented your MVP. Now it’s time to create some future Epics which represent the upcoming business outcomes you want to plan for the Product. Sprint planning in the context of iterating a Live Product involves determining and prioritizing the tasks to be tackled over a short, defined period (often 2 weeks) to enhance the Product, correct issues, or introduce new features.

Teams discuss and select work items based on their urgency, importance, and alignment with overall Product strategy, ensuring the Product continues to meet user needs and remains competitive. Iterating on a live Product is crucial to adapt to changing market conditions, user feedback, and technological advancements, thereby continuously improving user experience, addressing emerging issues, and delivering value to both the business and the end-users.

Below is a suggestive sprint plan that spans 2 weeks. You can use this same process to iterate your Live Product, and/or tailor it to the specific needs and layout of your team.

## Sprint Duration: 2 weeks (bi-weekly)

## Sprint Schedule:

## Sprint Planning (Day 0):

Product Owner presents the prioritized backlog to the development team.
The development team estimates the effort required for each user story.
The team collectively commits to a set of user stories they believe they can complete in the 2-week sprint.

## Daily Standup Meetings (Day 1 to Day 10):

Held daily at the same time and place (virtually or in-person).
Each team member shares:
What they accomplished since the last standup.
What they plan to work on next.
Any obstacles or issues they are facing.
The standup is time-boxed to 15 minutes to keep it concise and focused.

## Backlog Grooming (Day 3 or 4):

The development team and Product Owner review and refine the upcoming user stories in the backlog.
Ambiguities are clarified, and acceptance criteria are discussed to ensure a shared understanding.
The goal is to have a well-prepared and prioritized backlog for the next sprint.

## Sprint Refinement (Day 5 or 6):

The team comes together to discuss and refine user stories for the next sprint.
Technical details, dependencies, and design considerations are discussed.
By the end of the refinement session, user stories are ready for development.

## Sprint Progress and Adaptation (Day 1 to Day 10):

The development team works on the user stories they committed to during sprint planning.
The Scrum Master facilitates the removal of any blockers or impediments.
If new tasks emerge, the team collaborates to adjust the sprint scope accordingly.

## Sprint Review (Day 11):

The development team showcases the completed work to stakeholders, including the Product Owner and other relevant parties.
Feedback is gathered, and stakeholders have an opportunity to ask questions.
The Product Owner may reprioritize the backlog based on the feedback received.

## Sprint Retrospective (Day 11 or 12):

The development team reflects on the sprint and identifies what went well and areas for improvement.
Actionable items are discussed and recorded to enhance team Productivity and collaboration.
The Scrum Master helps facilitate the retrospective and ensures the team’s feedback is heard.

## Sprint Wrap-up and Planning for Next Sprint (Day 12 or 13):

The team finalizes any outstanding documentation or tasks from the current sprint.
Lessons learned and improvements identified during the retrospective are incorporated into the next sprint planning.
The cycle begins again with a new sprint planning meeting.
Note: when planning future sprints and deciding which User Stories should go next, you can use this Product Decision Matrix to score each item, with the higher-scored items going higher up in the backlog/sprint priority.

By following this 2 -week Agile Scrum process, you and your development team can efficiently plan, execute, and continuously improve on your process, as well as the Product. In this way, you can deliver valuable increments of the Product every two weeks. If you want to learn more, take this free SCRUM Fundamentals Course.

For a delivered release to be deemed successful, it must attain the following objectives:

- It’s deployed on time.
- It’s deployed within budget.
- It has little to no impact on current users.
- It satisfies the needs of current and new users, technological advances and/or competitive demands.

To achieve this, a Release Workflow process should be setup between Product and Engineering, which includes the following 6 stages:

## 1) Request

When feature requests come up on the Trello board, evaluate it for e.g. its rationale, feasibility, whether there’s a way to fulfil it by reconfiguring the application version already in Production. If the request makes it to the Strategic Roadmap as per the process outlined, this deliverable will make it into the Plan phase.

## 2) Plan

Write out the User Story as a ticket in the associated project management environment. Be sure to also include the User Story’s Background (context), Acceptance Criteria (specific requirements). Then, assign it for the most appropriate Sprint according to i) the Strategic Roadmap’s timing and ii) your Engineering team’s velocity.

## 3) Design and Build

This is the development phase where the ticket is converted to code. Ensure that you have an instant, clear line of communication with your Engineering team e.g. project management tool’s notifications / Slack / MS Teams so that you can respond to requests for clarification during this phase.

## 4) Testing

Once the release is deemed ready for testing, it’s deployed to a test/Dev/Staging environment where it’s subjected to non-functional and functional testing (including user acceptance testing or UAT). Your Engineers may move the ticket to the “QA” column in your project management tool and assign it to you for your review. If bugs are found, it’s sent back to them for tweaking and subjected to testing again. This iterative process continues until the release is cleared for Production deployment by both the development team and the Product Manager.

## 5) Deployment

The release is pushed to the Live/Production environment and made available to users.

## 6) Post-Deployment: Release Notes & Training

Post-deployment, Strategy and/or Product teams may want to educate users/sales/marketing via e.g. Demo’s on the changes, training them on how to operate the Product in the context of the new features.

Release Notes are also an essential part of the delivery. These will either be emailed to all users or posted in the Product’s Champions Teams Channel (example). Ideally, there should be a designated page within the Product for users to access the Release Notes.

## Updates and Announcements

The Product is bound to have a peak of users upon its release/announcement, and then trough when the agency users have seen what it is all about, or when they aren’t reminded of its existence.
