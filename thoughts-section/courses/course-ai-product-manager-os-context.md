## The context: the old pipeline worked - until the material changed

For decades, product teams ran a linear pipeline - **Idea → Design → Build → Test → Ship** - because the material was mostly deterministic. You could specify behavior, implement it, QA it, and trust it to behave.

AI changes the physics.

When the system’s core behavior is **probabilistic**, stage-gates stop protecting you:

- Product Requirement Documents can’t fully specify “correct.”
- Demos don’t predict production.
- Model updates shift behavior underneath you.
- “Ship” becomes an ongoing relationship with a living system.

This course is the continuation of the traditional Product Innovation Process - its **successor operating system**.

## The stable foundation: the 5-layer stack (how to think clearly)

Models change fast. Your operating system shouldn’t.

This course uses a stable 5-layer stack so you can locate any AI product problem in the right place:

- **Model**  -  capability, latency, cost; what the base model can and can’t do
- **Context**  -  prompts, RAG, memory, knowledge; what the model can *see*
- **Orchestration**  -  agents, tools, workflows; how work gets done across steps
- **Governance**  -  evals, guardrails, observability; how quality stays safe and measurable
- **Human**  -  vision, empathy, taste, communication, judgment; what cannot be delegated

## The new approach: replace the pipeline with a loop (what you do day-to-day)

Instead of pushing work through a line, you learn to run a tight loop:

**Talk** (Human + Context) **→ Decide** (Human + Governance) **→ Build** (Model + Orchestration) **→ Observe** (Governance) **→ Iterate** (all layers)

You don’t just learn concepts - you learn a repeatable way of working that holds up when output isn’t guaranteed.

## The course spine (the one argument you’re learning)

Everything in this course is organized around one property and what it forces:

- **Property:** outputs are probabilistic, not deterministic.
- **Four consequences:**
    1. You can’t “prompt and hope” → you must understand the model and load context.
    2. Probabilistic systems require loops, not straight lines.
    3. Probabilistic systems require evals + guardrails, not hope.
    4. Probabilistic systems change what being a professional means.

Each lesson “installs” one layer of the stack and explains one consequence  for each (with Lesson 2 planting the full map).

3. The Loop runs on a **5-Layer Stack** — *Model → Context → Orchestration → Governance → Human.*
4. The reason the process had to change is one property: **the material is probabilistic, not deterministic.** Four consequences fall out of that property, and each consequence is what a given lesson installs.

# Old Process vs. New Process

| **Old: Product Innovation Process** | **New: AI-Native Loop** |
| --- | --- |
| Idea | **Talk** |
| Design | **Decide** |
| Concept | **Build** |
| Alpha / Beta | **Observe** |
| Live | **Iterate** |

The old shape was linear because the material was deterministic — you could specify it, build it, ship it, and trust it to behave. The new shape is circular because the material is probabilistic — you have to *talk* to it, *decide* against judgment, *build* cheaply, *observe* what actually happened, and *iterate* the context back in. Same five steps as a shape — completely different physics underneath.

The Loop is measured in hours, not quarters. It is not a productivity framework. It is the physical consequence of building on probabilistic material.

# The 5-Layer Stack (what the Loop runs on)

Every turn of the Loop uses the Stack. The Stack itself does not change; only what sits inside each layer changes. That is what makes the course's promise durable — a cabinet that holds for the rest of your career, not a toolkit that decays in six months.

| Layer | What it is | How fast it changes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Human Layer** | Vision, empathy, taste, communication, judgment | Barely ever |
| **Governance Layer** | Evals, guardrails, observability, trust | Slow |
| **Orchestration Layer** | Agents, MCPs, workflow automation, the fleet | Medium |
| **Context Layer** | Company/product/user knowledge, RAG, memory, prompts | Medium |
| **Model Layer** | GPT, Claude, Gemini — the LLMs themselves | Fastest |

**Verb-to-Layer mapping (canonical, never drifts):**

- **Talk** — Human + Context. Asking a question, pulling on context, shaping what the model can see.
- **Decide** — Human + Governance. Making a judgment call against a standard of what's good.
- **Build** — Model + Orchestration. The model generates; orchestration makes it reach your data and tools.
- **Observe** — Governance. You measure the output against an eval set.
- **Iterate** — all five at once. You feed what you learned back into the Context Layer so the next loop runs on a richer foundation.

The verbs tell you *what* to do. The Stack tells you *how to think* while you are doing it. **Orchestration is the substrate that makes the Loop physically possible** — without MCPs, agents, and workflow wiring, the Loop is a whiteboard diagram.

# The Fundamental Property and Its Four Consequences

The property that forces everything above: **the output is probabilistic, not deterministic.** The model is not a compiler. It is a pattern-prediction machine. You cannot specify it, you cannot unit-test it the old way, and you cannot just prompt-and-hope.

Four consequences fall out of that single property. Each consequence maps directly onto the layer(s) installed in the corresponding lesson. This is the second spine of the course — the through-line that turns V3–V6 from a list of topics into a structured argument the viewer is walking across.

| Consequence | Claim | Related Layers | Lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **One** | Because the output is probabilistic, you can't just send the model a prompt and hope — you have to understand the model, and you have to load its context. | Model + Context | **Lesson 2** (Model) & **Lesson 3** (Context) |
| **Two** | Probabilistic systems need loops, not straight lines. | Orchestration | **Lesson 4** |
| **Three** | Probabilistic systems need guardrails and evals, not hope. | Governance | **Lesson 5** |
| **Four** | Probabilistic systems change what being a professional means. | Human | **Lesson 6** |

Every lesson from L2 onward opens by naming which consequence it is cashing in, and closes by reminding the viewer how many remain. L1 is the diagnosis (no consequence covered). L2 plants all four. L3 cashes the Context half of consequence one. L4 cashes two. L5 cashes three. L6 closes the arc.
